Recently I got to see the rumour (well, not really) about the highly anticipated plan to extend the reach of the digital distribution platform [Steam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_(service)), as well as PC gaming, with Valve's plan to put their first firm step onto the Arm 64-bit microarchitecture. Valve has confirmed their new [Steam Frame](https://store.steampowered.com/sale/steamframe), which is a [SteamOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SteamOS)-powered (Arch Linux-based) VR headset using a Snapdragon 8 Gen 3 SoC. Realistically this is equivalent to a high-end premium Android device. 

But the device alone isn't going to make it. What powers it from underneath the hardware turns out to also gain much attention from tech-savvy nerds. Since Steam Frame was introduced to be able to play PC games independently, it suggests that the SteamOS as well as the Steam client itself, which is the main character in the play, will eventually be ported over to Arm64, also with the help of *Proton* and *FEX*, two compatibility layers playing the role of, respectively, running Windows only games on Linux, and running most games which are primarily developed upon x86 (-64), on the promising Arm 64-bit systems. 

Valve hasn't ever formally announced the release of a native Steam client build before, the only way to run all Steam games and the client itself was from a translation layer called *Box86/Box64*, which is what FEX also do.
